    • History
  • Guam has a long and complex history, influenced by various cultures and colonial powers. Originally settled by the Chamorro people over 4,000 years ago, the island developed a distinct culture centered on seafaring, fishing, and agriculture. Spanish explorer Ferdinand Magellan arrived in 1521, and Guam was later claimed by Spain in 1565, becoming a key stopover on the trade route between Mexico and the Philippines. After more than 300 years under Spanish rule, the island was ceded to the United States in 1898 following the Spanish-American War. During World War II, Guam was occupied by Japanese forces for nearly three years until it was liberated by the U.S. in 1944. Today, Guam is a U.S. territory with a vibrant Chamorro culture and significant strategic importance.

    • Tourist Attraction
  • The number one tourist attraction in Guam is Tumon Bay, known for its crystal-clear waters, white sandy beaches, and vibrant marine life. Tumon Bay is the heart of Guam’s tourism, offering resorts, restaurants, shopping, and water activities like snorkeling, scuba diving, and paddleboarding. The bay is also home to Tumon’s Underwater World, a popular aquarium where visitors can walk through a tunnel surrounded by tropical fish, rays, and sharks.

    • Fun Fact
  • A fun fact about Guam is that it is home to Latte Stones, unique stone pillars that served as ancient foundations for Chamorro homes. These stones, found only in the Mariana Islands, have become a national symbol of Guam and represent the strength and resilience of the Chamorro people.

Guams Flavours

Guam’s cuisine, known as Chamorro food, combines traditional island flavors with influences from Spanish, Filipino, and American cuisines. A staple is red rice, colored and flavored with achiote seeds, often served with grilled meats like barbecue chicken, pork ribs, or beef kelaguen (a dish of chopped meat marinated in lemon juice, green onions, and hot peppers). Coconut, bananas, and breadfruit are also common ingredients. A beloved dessert, apigigi, is made from grated coconut and cassava, wrapped in banana leaves and grilled. Guam’s food is flavorful, hearty, and reflects the island’s multicultural heritage.

The Must Try Dish I Discovered in Guam

KELAGUEN

Kelaguen is a group of Chamorro dishes that are traditionally served either as a main course, an appetizer, or a side dish. Seafood, fish, or meat are typically marinated in a combination of lemon juice, salt, and hot peppers, which were introduced to the Marianas during the Spanish period. Chicken and fish kelaguen is often enriched with grated fresh coconut, and at Chamorro celebrations, kelaguen is served according to the ingredients used in it, so seafood is traditionally served after barbecued fish, while chicken kelaguen follows barbecued meat.
